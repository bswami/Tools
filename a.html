Making TCP Work

Well the first difference was the fact that me and 29 other technical people of GTO-I were invited for this workout - of the techies,for the techies,by the techies kind of deal. While this sounds like a no-brainer in hindsight, the HR claimed that this has not been attempted before. There was clear engagement from the GM and his leadership team. And this probably is a more practical way of implementing something like TCP in a diverse global organization like GE Healthcare - figure out the best bets for a specific pole and drive it through instead of trying to make grandiose plans and ripping out our hair on how to implement them uniformly across the poles.

Unlike some of the other workouts I had attended earlier, there were things to be accomplished before we eneded up at the off-site workout location. And the TODO list was actually quite long as we realized mid-way into our preparations. The 30 of us were divided into 3 teams and each was handed out a topic to prepare and provide recommendations. The topics were

1. Re-imagine GTO-I Technology Banquet - impact to be seen within 3 months

2. Imagine Technology Symposium - impact to be seen in 6-9 months time frame

3. (Wildly) Dream up technology culture - this hopefully sustains/supplements the impact created the first two points.

I was placed in the third team. As a first step, we started on figuring out what technology culture means - what are the attributes

that define tech. culture in an organization. After lot of intense brainstorming and discussions spread across several hours and days and

conference rooms, we came up with a list of 11 attributes. Each of us then went back and met with 5-10 members in our respective teams

tried to gather some inputs on the current state of affairs and possible suggestions for improving on each of those attributes.

On Oct 15th, we had a pre-work out report out to our management where we presented the work done so far. Few points were picked from the presentation we made and we were asked to focus on the following -

1. How to increase Domain knowledge

2. How to be in touch technology trends

3. How to create a technical pipeline

We went back to the drawing boards and spent some more hours locked up in a conference room in coming up with our recommendations for the three topics above. The final work out was scheduled for Oct 25 and 26 where we and the other two teams had to present their final set of recommendations.

I will cover the details on what happened at the work-out and subsequently in the next post.

What would have been your recommendations for fostering a technology culture within our organization ?

Technical Carrer Path (TCP) has been sold to engineers as the path to salvation. Unfortunately, attempts to define the structure and elements of that path and laying down the path itself has been a stumbling block for many organizations. Honey, we shrunk the MR scanner !

MR scanners have been moving towards higher and higher field strengths. One of the programs that I am working for right now is DVMR which is a 3 Tesla system. I have also heard of research systems that GE has developed that go upto 7T. So when I read this paper abstract that had the first images of a human brain taken with a an Ultra Low Field (ULF) MRI system with field strengths of a few μT, I was blown away. Detection at such low field strengths is being made possible because of Superconducting quantum interference devices or SQUIDs.

(I first heard of squids as marine creatures. Then this name got used for a popular web server cache proxy and now for MR detectors - search engines are going to have a real tough time in the future)

Current MR systems fill up couple of rooms with one for the magnet bore itself. Hospitals take months to plan out a new MR installation. This technology claims to shrink the size of a MR scanner to be trasported in a suitcase !

Will this prove to be the first signs of creative destruction for conventional MRI systems ?

That's what researchers at the Los Alamos Labs seem to be claiming. The place that gave us the H-Bomb is now promising to bring in the revolution of a suitcase sized MR scanner !

Swing debugging woes

Last week I faced a Catch-22 situation while debugging a Java Swing application. A colleague of mine reported some problems with the UI not displaying some values. I suggested using a debugger. We restarted the JVM with the debug parameters set and used Eclipse to connect to the JVM. We navigated to the method that we were interested in and set a break point. So far so good. The UI has 3 tables - we had to select a row in one of the tables and step through the code. The UI had one row already selected. Every time we switched from the Eclipse window to the Swing UI window, the screen would become blank ! The issue was that the Swing EDT was blocked at the breakpoint we had set in the code. When we navigate to the Eclipse window and say return, the UI would paint completely but when we switched back to the UI, it would become blank again ! The breakpoint was in the call made by getValueAt call of JTable and this seemed to be causing this annoying problem. On every paint request the JTable makes a getValueAt call for each column and then renders the column.

A killer blow

This post should be under the 'Did you know' category. I learnt something new today. oom-killer is its name. I was at the receiving end of this kernel behaviour.

Not me in person but a component that I own called event router. Few months back an issue was report from a MR target where event router seems to go down abruptly. I looked at the logs back then and did not find anything that could have caused the process to go down. Last week a similar issue was seen again. Today I got a mail with the link to the logs and the system's IP address. The logs were not much useful again. I logged into the system and looked at /var/log/messages to see if there were any clues. That's when I found this deadly message Jan 9 15:35:14 t10 kernel: oom-killer: gfp_mask=0xd1, order=0 A few more lines down the logfile an even more deadlier message Jan 9 15:35:15 t10 kernel: Out of Memory: Killed process 756 (java). Googling for oom-killer revealed that this is the out of memory(oom) management routine in the kernel which kicks in when the system runs out of virtual memory. The "badness" function within oom-killer does some math with the amount of memory used by various processes, its nice value etc and picks a candidate for slaying. And wham - it just puts the process out of existence ! Event router, for a reason yet unknown , found itself under the guillotine. I did see some bug reports for kernels 2.6.15 and below where oom-killer starts off even when system has enough virtual memory. I think that this bug is what caused the issue that was seen in the MR target. Anyways some more investigations are needed before we close on this one. A very interesting find for the day. Anytime you find your process vanishing in thin air - look for oom-killer in /var/log/messages :-)

Engineers Weak ?

This week is the National Engineer's Week in the U.S. There are various events being planned in our organization as well. This got me thinking on whether software makes the cut as an engineering discipline or not. The jury is still out - in my humble opinion. There are basically 2 different schools of thought. One which is trying to bring the rigor of other engineering domains into software development (e.g. CMMi). And the other which is trying to find new ways to make software development more predictable and error free without necessarily looking at existing engineering practices (e.g. Agile) . Probably the higher level goal of both these approaches are the same - just their way of getting there is different. I still feel that there is a long way to go for software development practices to evolve and standardize so that it starts to feel like engineering.

Design for Service - a very simple example

I had to change the inverter battery at my home about six months back - the older one was starting to impact the UPS performance. I went to a Exide showroom and ended up buying a battery made specifically for inverters (my earlier battery was an automobile batter). The price was much higher compared to automotive batteries but I was told that this would perform much better as it was designed for home use. There was a specific design feature that really impressed me. These were the "float guides" sitting on top of the battery.

These float guides are basically a service feature - they tell the user when it is time to add distilled water and save the battery from getting corroded by the acid. The glass tubes start sinking in and when they reach below the lower red mark, that is when you have to act. Now this looks pretty straightforward - you don't need a degree in electrical/mechanical or any engineering for that matter to understand how this works. But none of the batteries that I had seen earlier had this feature. I was totally bowled over by the simplicity of this idea. This is a great example of a simplistic innovation that has a great impact on product life. I wish our products had such simple design features that would enable "real" proactive service.

We have many validators available for checking DICOM files but all of the them run stand-alone. This may be fine for some scenarios, but I thought that it would be generally useful to have a simple,fast and "highly" available validator for DICOM images - something similar to the HTML validator hosted by W3C. This would also be exposed as a web service so other applications could make use of it. And what better place to host this service than the Google App Engine. I was lucky to find a GPL pure python DICOM parser that I plan to use in this project. I have started by implementing all the validation rules for different VR's as specified in the DICOM standard Part 5. I am starting off with VR validation and planning to add type validation in the next phase. Once that is done, I will build the front end that will accept a DICOM file, run it through the validator and publish a report on the issues found. You can monitor progress of this project on the chart that I have added to this blog on the right (which, by the way, uses the Google Chart API's).

Digging deeper

Just thought of giving a little peek into the details of the DICOM validator. There are 27 VR's (Value Representation) defined in the DICOM standard. VR's are nothing but data types that values in a DICOM image can have. For example, PN (Person Name) is a VR that values like patient names should be stored in. Often times there are issues in 2 systems exchanging information in DICOM because some tag in the image does not have data encoded according to the VR specification. Isolation of such problems can be time consuming - being in a DICOM platform team, I have personally lost sleep (and some color of my hair) debugging these problems. Python is what the app engine supports as the runtime language. So I am building classes, one for each VR, that implement the rules as mentioned in Table 6.2-1 in Part 5 of the DICOM standard. For each class, there is also a unit test class that checks with various inputs (both good and bad) and verfies that the output is as expected. The app engine seems to have been designed with the intention of solving a rival framework, Ruby on Rails, weakest spot - scalability. Why python ? Why not get RoR to run on the Google infrastructure. I think it is because Guido works for Google; Matz does'nt.

On Demand Radiology

Call it Radiology as a Service (RaaS) - just to coin a new acronym. GEHC has a diverse portfolio of products serving various segments and clinical domains. When it comes to emerging markets such as India, the higher price of our best offerings become a deterrent to many customers. While we have managed to garner a decent market share with some of the bigger hospital chains in India and introduced innovative products such as the XR6000 systems, there is still a huge opportunity that we can leverage. So here's the idea :

  1. Set up a web based reservation system - customers will be able to login and "build their own radiology department". We can organize our products around care areas (cardiology,oncology etc) and allow customers to select products of their choice.
  2. Based on the selection and duration of need (days,weeks or months) we calculate the cost. If customer likes the price, he confirms the reservation
  3. Based on our resource availability we get back to the customer - if we can meet the need, we basically send a truck with the selected products on it and drive down to the customer's place. The truck is parked there for the required duration.
  4. As value added services, we can send our techs and FE's - so the customer only needs to focus on the patient.
The pricing model is where we can make this attractive to an otherwise intimidated (by the prospect of actually buying all that equipment) customer. A fixed cost depending on the products selected and the number of days of service required. And a variable component based on the number of procedures actually performed. If we can follow the Apple AppStore model - if the hospital decides to waive off all procedure charges to the patient, we can do away with the variable component. If I can build a good business case (with numbers), then this can pitched to one of the business leaders. That is way forward for this idea in my mind. Update
  1. This can be targeted at existing customers who may want to augment their existing radiology department for short a short duration to manage demand peaks.
  2. While proposed as an emerging market concept, there is no reason why this should not be used in developed markets, especially considering the recessionary pressures that everyone is facing.
  3. We can spin this off as a separate business itself and run it like a franchisee model where we can invite entrepreneurs to run the show with GEHC providing the necessary support.

Deterministic Parallel Programs

I gave a talk on "Concurrency Oriented Programming" at the HCT India Technology Symposium in November 2010. One of the areas I have been interested and keenly following technology developments is concurrent programming. With the omnipresent trend in increase in multi-core processors (including smart phones now), the expectations around software performance gains are growing as well. Unfortunately most of the current breed of software has not been designed for exploiting this new computing power. From software for healthcare systems perspective, another aspect that is critical is to have defect free software. This becomes difficult to guarantee with parallel programs as the current programming models (threads and shared memory being the most popular) are not deterministic. An interesting development in this space is the research project at UIUC called Deterministic Parallel Java (DPJ) provides a framework for building software that is not only scalable but also deterministic. So not only will the programs run faster with more number of cores, they will also be free from typical "parallel" bugs of data races and deadlocks. Success of such projects will determine how successful we, as software engineers, are in making best of use of the tremendous computing power that will be at our disposal in the next few years.

Metrica gratis metricus ?

Had an interesting discussion last evening with a colleague around coupling metrics reported by Sonar. The specific metric under discussion was related to coupling. Spring based class initialization with a number of attributes ( > 10) was causing Sonar to flag the class as above the permissible limit of coupling for a class viz. 10. Now this class is coming out of standards (FHIR) based model - so there is not much that can be done there. The question that comes up is whether the metric makes sense for modern code generation scenarios where class mapping is automated and not directly in the control of the developer. XSD, Model driven architecture are couple of scenarios where one is bound to encounter this often.A few suggestions that I had

  • Justify the exception if the attributes are pure bean classes with no other behavior
  • Group attributes to composite objects that are then initialized
  • Have a tolerance limit instead of absolute number - 10 + or - 5 instead of an absolute number - 10
Which leads us to the question that is the blog's title. Do metrics exist only for metrics sake ? How do we tailor the process to deal with generated code scenarios ?

Microservices

Wish the concept was named better. One of the first things that any of the practitioners say when describing microservices is the fact that term micro in the name is a misnomer. It has nothing to do with the "size" of the service - in terms or number of end points or number of classes etc. It is all about slicing the system vertically instead of horizontally. Along business capabilities to help deliver value more rapidly. Bounded Context , a concept from Domain Driven Design is a possible approach to partition the system and identifying microservices. Anti-fragile - opposite of fragile as opposed to robust or resilient. Anti-fragile means that the system gets better on encountering failures. Microservices architecture enable systems to be anti-fragile by ensuring that the entire system does not grind to a halt when one of the services encounters problems. Implications to team organization include having full stack developers in single team instead of separate scrum team for front end vs. those for database and third one for business services.
